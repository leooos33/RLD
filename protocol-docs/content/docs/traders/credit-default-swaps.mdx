# Credit Default Swaps

Protocol insolvency is DeFi's largest unpriced tail risk. Every time you supply assets to Aave, Morpho, or any lending pool, you're effectively selling insurance against the protocol going bust — and you're not getting paid for it.

RLD Credit Default Swaps (CDS) let you explicitly price and trade this risk. Buy protection to hedge your deposits, or sell protection to earn a premium for taking on tail risk.

---

## The Problem: Hidden Risk in Lending

When you supply USDC to a lending protocol, you earn yield. What most users don't realize is that this yield includes an *invisible* insurance premium baked in.

**What can go wrong:**
- A collateral asset depegs (e.g., stETH loses peg → mass bad debt).
- A smart contract exploit drains the pool.
- An oracle failure triggers cascading liquidations.
- Utilization hits 100% and you can't withdraw (bank run).

In all of these scenarios, the lending pool accumulates **bad debt** — debt that can never be repaid. As a supplier, your deposits absorb this loss. You are, in options terminology, **short a put option** on every collateral asset in the pool.

Traditional finance has credit default swaps to manage this exact risk.

---

## How RLD CDS Works

RLD CDS is **parametric** — it doesn't require anyone to vote on whether a "credit event" happened. Instead, it uses the lending pool's own interest rate model as a deterministic trigger.

**The insight:** When a lending pool is under severe stress (depeg, exploit, bank run), utilization spikes to 100% and the borrowing rate rockets to its maximum. This rate spike is a deterministic, on-chain signal that something is very wrong.

### Step-by-Step: Buying Protection

Imagine you hold 100k USDC on a lending protocol and want to insure it against default.

**Step 1: Open a CDS Position**
1. You navigate to the CDS market and select your coverage amount (e.g., $100k).
2. The protocol mints a Long CDS position for you.
3. To keep this position active, you pay a continuous funding premium. This premium is automatically deducted from your margin account over time.

**Step 2: Monitoring the Trigger (Normal Markets)**
While the lending pool is healthy, utilization is normal (e.g., 60-80%), and the borrowing rate is stable (e.g., 5-10%). 
- Your CDS position remains inactive in terms of payout. 
- You simply pay the ongoing premium, just like paying a monthly insurance bill.

**Step 3: The Crisis Event (The Trigger)**
A massive exploit occurs on the lending protocol. Depositors panic and try to withdraw their funds.
- As funds leave, the pool's utilization skyrockets to **100%**.
- Because there is no capital left to borrow or withdraw, the lending protocol's algorithmic interest rate model defensively spikes the borrowing rate to its absolute maximum (e.g., 100% APY or higher).
- **This maximum rate spike is the objective, on-chain trigger.**

**Step 4: Automatic Payout**
Because your CDS position is mathematically indexed to the borrowing rate, its value explodes upward precisely as the rate spikes.
- The underwriter's collateral is automatically transferred into your position's value.
- You can now sell your CDS position to realize the cash payout.
- The payout scales linearly with the rate: if the rate hits its defined maximum, you receive 100% of your insured notional.

### Step-by-Step: Selling Protection (Underwriting)

If you believe a lending pool is safe, you can act as the insurance provider and earn the premiums.

**Step 1: Lock Collateral**
You deposit USDC into your margin account and open a Short CDS position. Your USDC is locked as collateral to guarantee you can pay out buyers if a crisis happens.

**Step 2: Collect Premium**
In normal markets, you continuously receive funding premiums from protection buyers. 
- Unlike standard lending yields (which you only earn from borrowers), CDS premiums scale dynamically. 
- As pool utilization creeps up and risk increases, the premium you collect automatically accelerates. This ensures you are paid more when the system is under stress.

**Step 3: Settlement in a Crisis**
If the lending protocol fails and the rate hits maximum, your Short CDS position loses value.
- Your locked collateral is mathematically transferred to the protection buyers.
- Your maximum loss is strictly capped at the collateral you committed. You can never lose more than your initial deposit.

## Why Parametric?

Traditional insurance  requires human governance to decide if a "claim event" happened. This introduces:
- **Delay** — Claims take weeks to resolve.
- **Subjectivity** — Committees can deny legitimate claims.
- **Counterparty risk** — The insurance fund itself might be insolvent.

Parametric CDS eliminates all three:
- **Instant** — The rate model triggers automatically.
- **Objective** — No human discretion. Math decides.
- **Fully collateralized** — Underwriter capital is locked on-chain.

## Covered Risks

| Risk Event | How CDS Detects It |
| --- | --- |
| **Stablecoin depeg** | Collateral value drops → liquidations fail → bad debt → utilization spikes |
| **Smart contract exploit** | Funds drained → remaining depositors can't withdraw → utilization → 100% |
| **Oracle failure** | Stale/manipulated prices → cascading liquidations → bad debt |
| **Bank run** | Mass withdrawals → utilization spikes → rate hits maximum |
| **LST/LRT slashing** | Collateral value drops suddenly → Poisson jump event |

In every scenario, the common signature is the same: **utilization → 1, rate → maximum**. The CDS is calibrated to this universal distress signal.

## The Economics

**Why does underwriting yield beat supply yield?**

As a lender, you earn the supply rate but bear the full default risk. As a CDS underwriter, you earn a premium that scales with the *borrow rate* — which is always higher than the supply rate due to the utilization spread.

During normal markets, the underwriter's premium equals the borrow rate scaled by how close utilization is to maximum. This means:
- In calm markets (low utilization): small, steady premium.
- As stress builds (rising utilization): premium accelerates convexly.
- At maximum stress: premium matches the maximum borrow rate.

This convex scaling means underwriters are naturally compensated *more* precisely when risk is highest — the opposite of traditional insurance where premiums are fixed regardless of conditions.
